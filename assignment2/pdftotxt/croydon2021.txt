Executive Summary 
1 
INTRODUCTION 
1.1 
In the current climate, a major new cyber security breach globally occurs on a 
daily basis.  Even at large, well-protected organisations, successful cyber-
attacks have far reaching economic, reputational, and legal consequences for 
organisations. 
1.2 
Ineffective controls over information security and cyber awareness could lead 
to a compromise of the IT infrastructure, which in turn could lead to a loss of 
data, and possible legal/regulatory breaches (e.g. GDPR).  The Council has an 
internal IT/Digital team but continues to be reliant on third party IT service 
providers including a legacy (albeit reducing in size) provision from Capita, 
which supports the network and firewall infrastructures and more recently 
providers such as Littlefish, which provide local desktop and helpdesk support.  
Cyber Security is the responsibility of an internal member of staff but again 
some of the controls are dependent on Capita and Littlefish. 
1.3 
This audit will be split into two stages as follows: 
 Cyber Security Governance and Protection – this phase of the audit will occur 
during 2020-21 financial year and will leverage an anonymised set of key 
findings (good practices and areas for improvement) from Mazars internal IT 
audits of cyber security since the beginning of 2020 and the emerging themes 
arising from the Covid-19 lockdowns.  This will primarily focus on Cyber related 
governance and protective controls.  The objective being to focus the audit 
specifically on key themes noted elsewhere which are common across peer 
groups; and 
 Cyber Security Detection, Response and Resolution – this phase of the audit 
will (a) follow up the findings from the above phase and (b) focus on Cyber 
related detective, response and resolution controls and will be conducted during 
2021-22 financial year. 
1.4 
This audit addresses the first of the two stages and is being undertaken as part 
of the agreed Internal Audit Plan for 2020/21.  The fieldwork for this review was 
completed during the government measures put in place in response to Covid-
19. Consequently, testing was performed remotely. However, we have been 
able to obtain all relevant documents required for the review and cover all 
planned areas in the scope remotely

Audit Area: Penetration Testing 
Priority Action Proposed by Management 
Detailed Finding/Rationale – Issue 1 
1 
We will conduct penetration testing 
within the 2021/22 financial year. 
The Council has not undertaken recent penetration testing across the network and 
infrastructures. Such testing is commonly undertaken by the Council’s peers at least 
annually and more frequently if significant changes are proposed to the IT environment.    
Penetration testing is an authorised test of a system, network or infrastructure designed 
to identify security vulnerabilities/weaknesses so that these can be fixed.  It is a core tool 
for analysing security but should not be considered a ‘magic bullet’ as the test can only 
validate that your organisation’s systems, network or infrastructures are not vulnerable to 
known issues on the day of the test.  Therefore, it is also important for any organisation 
that undertakes penetration testing to follow up on the results of the test, ensuring that 
action plans are developed and implemented for detected vulnerabilities.

Deadline 
The Council is still in an early stage of embedding its cyber security hygiene 
and has a clear plan for the steps and issues that it needs to address. Although 
recently there has not been penetration testing we have implemented regular 
vulnerability scanning and reporting and are aiming for a full IT health check 
and pen test within 2021/22

newsletters and emails but these tend to be unstructured and not necessarily formally

Many organisations including those in local government have or plan to undertake regular 
‘phishing’ simulations with their staff, however, the Council has not undertaken or does 
not have plans to do the same. 
Phishing is an attack method adopted by hackers where untargeted, mass emails are sent 
to many people asking for sensitive information (such as bank details) or encouraging 
them to visit potentially fake websites.  This method has increased in use during the last 
18 months and has become commonly successful, most notably in the public sector and 
in local government particularly.  More than 90% of the cyber attacks and data breaches 
that occur are due to phishing emails as employees are often unable to differentiate 
between malicious and legitimate emails.

Audit Area: Privileged/administrator account reviews 
Priority Action Proposed by Management 
Detailed Finding/Rationale – Issue 6 
2 
Continue with our cybsercurity work 
plan to address this issue throughout 
2021 
Privileged/administrator accounts across infrastructures have been reviewed in the past, 
including most recently Active Directory Domain Admin reviews in March 2020 and Azure 
and Office365 admin reviews in August 2020. However, these are neither formally 
scheduled to occur at pre-determined frequencies nor undertaken regularly enough. 
Privileged access is the route to an organisation’s critical assets because they carry 
elevated rights that standard users do not commonly have.  Such elevated rights should 
be used only for justified purposes and in some organisations are only provided based on 
an incident or change ticket via the service desk.  It is acknowledged that such practices 
are not effective for some organisations in maintaining service delivery, therefore frequent 
reviews of those users with elevated rights helps ensure that only appropriate users have 
such access and only for as long as is necessary.  It is also noted that unused and/or 
dormant privileged accounts can be targeted by hackers and if compromised can be used 
to traverse the network as part of their reconnaissance.

Responsible Officer

Once we have completed the initial ‘clean-up’ phase of this process we will implement 
regular scheduled reviews of all privileged accounts across our estate. Work as also

access, leavers & starters, retention periods.

Audit Area: Firewall Rule Review 
Priority Action Proposed by Management 
Detailed Finding/Rationale – Issue 7 
2 
We will put this in place. 
There is no timeframe defined in any policy/documentation which triggers firewall rules 
which are no longer needed to be disabled or removed.   
As noted in Issue 1 above, penetration testing only identifies security vulnerabilities on 
the day of the test and therefore does not provide ongoing assurance that security 
vulnerabilities are not introduced and if so, are detected in a timely manner. 
Due to constant change and the growing number of threats facing organisations, firewall 
security must continuously adapt to combat existing threats.  Due to standards such as 
PCI-DSS and ISO27001, reviews of firewall rules are increasingly important.  Even if an 
organisation does not need to meet these standards, the organisation may need to 
demonstrate that the network is secure for relationships with specific partners or 
customers.

Appendix 2 
Definitions for Audit Opinions and Identified Issues 
In order to assist management in using our reports: 
We categorise our audit assurance opinion according to our overall assessment of the risk 
management system, effectiveness of the controls in place and the level of compliance with these 
controls and the action being taken to remedy significant findings or weaknesses. 
 
 
Full Assurance 
There is a sound system of control designed to achieve 
the system objectives and the controls are constantly 
applied. 
 
Substantial Assurance 
While there is basically a sound system of control to 
achieve the system objectives, there are 
weaknesses in the design or level of non-compliance 
of the controls which may put this achievement at 
risk. 
 
Limited Assurance 
There are significant weaknesses in key areas of 
system controls and non-compliance that puts 
achieving the system objectives at risk,   
 
No Assurance 
Controls are non-existent or extremely weak, leaving 
the system open to the high risk of error, abuse and 
reputational damage. 
 
Priorities assigned to identified issues are based on the following criteria: 
Priority 1 
(High) 
Fundamental control weaknesses that require immediate attention by 
management to action and mitigate significant exposure to risk. 
Priority 2 
(Medium) 
Control weakness that still represent an exposure to risk and need to be 
addressed within a reasonable period.  
Priority 3 
(Low) 
Although control weaknesses are considered to be relatively minor and low risk, 
still provides an opportunity for improvement.  May also apply to areas 
considered to be of best practice that can improve for example the value for 
money of the review area.
